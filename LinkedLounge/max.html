The Emptiness of Liberal Zionism

Every night I fall asleep under an Israeli flag. In high school I went to Israel with my family, and in college I went with my friends on Birthright. I wrote Op Eds, argued in classes and debates, all in support of Israeli and the Israeli state. While much of my politics have shifted – especially in recent years – further and further left, my Zionism has been one of the few constants.

Yet recently, I have found myself questioning even my Zionism. I carefully angle my camera whenever I am on a zoom call from my room so that my flag is no longer visible. I don’t have the same passion I once did from defending and supporting Israel. And I know that I am not alone – support for Israel among young Jews is on the decline. Increasingly, Birthright trips have found their propaganda interrupted by young Jews asking tough questions about the occupation. Questions about Zionism are mounting, and it appears that there are no answers.

The right-wing and religious arguments for Zionism have remained consistent – the Jews have a right to the land that makes up Israel, Palestinians do not, and there is nothing more to be said. Of course Jews, especially American Jews, are not generally conservative, but are overwhelmingly liberal. According to a 2013 Pew study, 70% of Jews identify as Democrats and 49% identify as liberal (as compared to 49% and 21% of the total US population respectively). For many of these Jews, the idea of an ethnostate is anathema in a way it generally isn’t to more conservative Jews. This conflict has fueled an entire industry devoted to advancing arguments for a liberal Zionism, seeking to merge liberal values with support for the State of Israel [1].

The leftist critique of liberal Zionism is that the term is an oxymoron – there can be no such thing as a liberal ethnostate, and there can be no such thing as a liberal state built upon colonialism. The leftist critique says that a close examination of liberal Zionism will always reveal that its name is a lie, and that Zionism is at its core a right-wing project that cannot be reconciled with liberalism. Perhaps the most straightforward statement of anti-Zionism came from UN General Assembly Resolution 3379, adopted in 1975 and revoked in 1991, which stated that “Zionism is a form of racism and racial discrimination.” The resolution also made reference to the now-commonplace comparison between South African Apartheid and Israeli treatment of Palestinians.

Right-wing Zionism is unbothered by this criticism, as it does not see racism as a problem. But this critique does present a serious problem for liberal Zionism, and liberal Zionism seems increasingly ill-equipped to answer it. Young Jews are intellectually curious, and they have spent most of their lives being told that Israel is good and just. But as they mature, it becomes increasingly impossible to square the rosy image of Israel they have been presented with – to borrow an Israeli term – the facts on the ground.

From a left-wing standpoint, current Israeli politics are indefensible. Israel denies Palestinians basic human rights and dignity. The occupation is a legal and a moral nightmare. And critically for Liberal Zionism, whatever argument exists for a more liberal version of the Israeli State has been soundly rejected by Israelis themselves. The Israeli left is well and truly dead; its labor party is a nonentity in the Knesset. And for those who think the kids will lead Israel to a brighter future, think again: young Israelis are more right-wing than their parents. It’s difficult to imagine a bleaker situation for the Israeli left than the present, but by far the most likely future is a politics further right than the present.

Current Israeli leadership has also dealt a death blow to one of the most common Zionist arguments, namely that Jewish self-determination is necessary for Jewish safety. Zionists of all stripes have long held that the only way to guarantee protection of Jews from Antisemitism is to allow Jews to govern themselves. But take a look at the closest allies of the Netanyahu regime: Donald Trump, Jair Bolsonaro, Viktor Orban, Narendra Modi. Fascists all, and none are true friends of Jews. If the price of Jewish sovereignty is strengthening and allying with fascists – a political ideology that, to put things mildly, has not historically been friendly to Jews – what exactly is the point of having a Jewish state? How can Israel be said to make Jews any safer if it empowers and protects antisemities?

So if Israelis will not make the argument for liberal Zionism, it must fall to intellectuals and supporters of Liberal Zionism abroad. But actual, substantive defenses of liberal Zionism are nowhere to be found. A new group of young Zionist influencers has arisen online to take up the fight against anti-Zionism. Their language has even infected the ostensibly more serious Liberal Zionists, who now face much the same problem that modern American liberals face. Their arguments amount to nothing more than co-opting social justice language: claims that Jews are “indigenous” to Israel, that Palestinians are “colonizers.”

When they aren’t aping the language of leftists, modern Zionists have taken to simply labeling any type of critique of Israel as being equivalent to antisemtism. While it is true that antisemitism is often masked as antizionism, it seems that for many Zionists there is simply no way to criticize Israel without being antisemtic. Zionists swarmed Michael Che for making a relatively anodyne joke on a recent episode of Saturday Night Live. During the Weekend Update Segment, after reporting that Israel had vaccinated half of its population, Che riffed that he “was gonna guess that it’s the Jewish half.” The joke was clearly skewering the uncontested fact that Israel had not been providing vaccines to Palestinians (a moral outrage and a flagrant violation of international law), yet Zionists somehow concocted an argument that Che hated Jews. But at least in Che’s case there was an actual quote over which liberal Zionists could gin up fake outrage. In other cases, offenses are fabricated whole-cloth.

Take the recent furor over the state of California’s new ethnic studies curriculum. The Jewish publication Tablet wrote an entire article accusing the curriculum of being antisemtic, citing a pretty horrific quote contained within the curriculum. There was just one small problem. As David Schraub pointed out, the offending quote simply wasn’t in the curriculum. Thanks to the work of various Jewish organizations, the draft curriculum actually included lots of great content about Jewish life and culture. But instead of reflecting on what was ultimately a productive and beneficial process for Jews in an area – ethnic studies – that is often accused of being hostile to Jews.

These manufactured controversies suggest that liberal Zionists are afraid of engaging with their opponents on fair ground. They seem to know that an actual, substantive discussion about Israel could lead to places they would prefer not to go. In effect, the modern supporters of liberal Zionism have substantiated the arguments that leftists have long made against it – it is in fact a thin veneer providing otherwise liberal Jews with a cover for a right-wing belief.

At some point, I suspect, the shame will become overwhelming, and my Israeli flag will come down. And for all the screeching of the Zionist establishment, I will not have been swayed by my leftist college professors or by fear of being cancelled. I will have simply finally woken up to the intellectual emptiness and moral turpitude of liberal Zionism.
